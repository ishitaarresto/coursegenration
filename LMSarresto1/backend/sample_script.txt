Module 1: Understanding Road Risks

Lesson 1.1 — Why Road Safety Matters
Learning objectives: Recognize the leading causes of road accidents; understand that most crashes are preventable; identify your personal role in road safety.

Slide 1 — Title: "Why Road Safety Matters"
Subtitle: Every journey is a shared responsibility
Slide 2 — Heading: "The reality"
• Road crashes are among the leading causes of preventable injury worldwide
• Most are caused by human factors: speed, distraction, fatigue, impairment
• A single decision can change a life
Slide 3 — Heading: "Your role"
• Safety isn't only the driver's job — passengers, pedestrians, and cyclists all share the road
• Small habits prevent large tragedies

Audio narration script (≈45 seconds):
Every time we step onto a road — driving, walking, or cycling — we enter a shared space where our choices affect everyone around us. The hard truth is that the vast majority of road crashes are not accidents in the true sense. They are predictable, and they are preventable. Speed, distraction, fatigue, and impairment are behind most serious collisions. The encouraging side of that fact is this: because these causes come down to human decisions, they are within our control. Road safety begins not with rules, but with the choices each of us makes before and during every journey.

[0:20–0:35] Montage of "small good decisions" — buckling a seatbelt, putting a phone in the glovebox, checking mirrors. Text overlay: "Prevention is a choice."

Lesson 1.2 — Common Causes of Accidents
Learning objectives: Identify the four main human-factor causes of crashes; explain how each impairs safe driving; recognize these risks in everyday situations.

Slide 1 — Title: "Common Causes of Accidents"
Slide 2 — Heading: "The big four"
• Speeding — reduces reaction time and increases impact force
• Distraction — phones, eating, adjusting controls
• Fatigue — slows reactions, causes microsleeps
• Impairment — alcohol, drugs, some medications
Slide 3 — Heading: "Why they're dangerous"
• Each one steals the time and attention you need to respond
• They often combine — a tired, distracted driver is far higher risk

Audio narration script (≈40 seconds):
Most crashes trace back to four human factors. The first is speed — the faster you travel, the less time you have to react, and the harder you hit. The second is distraction; even a two-second glance at a phone means traveling blind across a surprising distance. Third is fatigue, which slows your reactions and can cause brief microsleeps you may not even notice. And fourth is impairment, whether from alcohol, drugs, or even certain medications. Each of these steals something essential — your time, your attention, or your judgment. And they rarely act alone.

[0:00–0:10] Four-panel split screen, each showing one cause: a speedometer climbing, a phone lighting up, drooping eyelids, a blurred bar scene.
[0:10–0:30] Each panel expands full-screen in turn as narration names it, with a clear text label.
[0:30–0:40] Panels recombine; one car icon visibly carries all four risk markers. Text overlay: "Risks multiply."

Module 2: Safe Practices for Everyone

Lesson 2.1 — Pedestrian and Cyclist Safety
Learning objectives: Apply safe crossing and visibility practices; understand cyclist road positioning; recognize shared-space responsibilities.

Slide 1 — Title: "Pedestrian & Cyclist Safety"
Slide 2 — Heading: "On foot"
• Use crossings and footpaths where available
• Make eye contact with drivers before stepping out
• Be visible — bright or reflective clothing at night
Slide 3 — Heading: "On two wheels"
• Always wear a helmet
• Signal turns clearly and ride predictably
• Use lights and reflectors after dark

Audio narration script (≈40 seconds):
Roads aren't only for vehicles. As a pedestrian, your safest habits are simple: use crossings and footpaths, and never assume a driver has seen you — make eye contact before you step out. Visibility saves lives, so wear bright or reflective clothing at night. As a cyclist, treat yourself as a vehicle. Wear a helmet on every ride, signal your turns clearly, and ride in a predictable line so drivers can anticipate you. After dark, lights and reflectors aren't optional — they're the difference between being seen and being invisible. The road is shared, and safety flows in every direction.

[0:12–0:25] Cyclist riding confidently in the correct road position, signaling a turn. Camera follows smoothly.
[0:25–0:40] Night scene transition: same pedestrian and cyclist, now lit up with reflective gear and lights, clearly visible against traffic. End card: "Be seen. Be safe."

Lesson 2.2 — Defensive Driving Basics
Learning objectives: Define defensive driving; apply the safe-following-distance rule; anticipate and respond to others' mistakes.

Slide 1 — Title: "Defensive Driving Basics"
Slide 2 — Heading: "What it means"
• Drive expecting others to make mistakes
• Stay aware, stay calm, stay ready
Slide 3 — Heading: "Core habits"
• Keep a safe following distance (the 3-second rule)
• Scan ahead and check mirrors regularly
• Adjust for weather, light, and road conditions

Audio narration script (≈40 seconds):
Defensive driving means one thing above all: drive as though everyone else might make a mistake — because sometimes they will. The goal isn't to assume the worst of others, but to leave yourself enough room and time to respond when something unexpected happens. The simplest tool is the three-second rule: pick a fixed point ahead, and make sure at least three seconds pass between the car in front reaching it and you reaching it. Add more in rain, fog, or darkness. Keep your eyes scanning far ahead, not just at the bumper in front, and check your mirrors often. Defensive driving turns surprises into manageable moments.

[0:10–0:22] Demonstration: car ahead brakes suddenly; the defensive driver has room and slows calmly. Contrast cut: a tailgating car forced to brake hard.
[0:22–0:35] Driver's eyes scanning far ahead, mirror checks shown with subtle highlight circles.
[0:35–0:45] Weather shift to rain; following distance visibly increases. End card: "Expect. Anticipate. Respond."
